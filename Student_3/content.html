Musical Periods: The History of Classical Music

We'll describe how music has developed significantly over the past thousand years. Gregorian Chants and Mozart's sonatas are just a few examples of how the 6 Musical Periods have impacted music today. The six musical eras are classified as Medieval, Renaissance, Baroque, Classical, Romantic, and 20th/21st Century, with each generally corresponding to a distinct time period.

Medieval (1150 – 1400)

The Medieval era is the first in which we can be certain of how music sounded at this time, even though we can assume that music started much earlier than 1150. Since the church or locations associated with the church produced the majority of the notated manuscripts from the Middle Ages, most of the works have a sacred theme.

Gregorian Chant is arguably the most well-known genre of music to have emerged during the Middle Ages. Monks sang Gregorian Chants, which were monophonic (consisting of a single, solo melodic line). Listen to the Gregorian Chant in the video below for a minute. Also take note of the backdrop notation, which has evolved significantly over time.

Renaissance (1400 – 1600)

Due to the majority of composers' attention being directed toward choral music, the Renaissance saw a substantial increase in harmony and polyphony in music.

Throughout the entirety of the Renaissance, religious music flourished, and new genres like masses, hymns, psalms, and motets were developed. Toward the conclusion of the era, some sacred music composers started utilizing secular forms (like the madrigal). Listen for a minute to Orlando Gibbons' well-known choral composition "The Silver Swan," which is still performed today.

The second part of the Renaissance had a significant impact on composers as they began to organize their works using major and minor scales rather than the modal harmony system.

Baroque (1600 – 1750)

The creation of writing music in a specific key dates back to the Baroque era, which built on the end of the Renaissance. However, the Baroque era is well known for its intricate rhythms and challenging compositions. However, this time set the stage for the following 300 years of music.

Along with opera, the concerto, sonata, and cantata, the concept of the contemporary orchestra was created. With the rise of instrumental works for different groups, instrumental music no longer reigned supreme over choral music. "Classical" music started to slowly permeate society, either as a spectacle in the shape of opera or outside at dinner parties and other special occasions.

Classical (1750 – 1820)

"Classical music" has two distinct definitions. The term's wider definition embraces all Western art music from the Middle Ages to the present. The particular reference is to music that was popular from the 1750s to the early 1820s. In this part, we'll go over the precise meaning. The Baroque era was enhanced by the Classical period, which also introduced the sonata, a highly influential new song form. The concerto, symphony, sonata, trio, and quintet were also developed during this time.

The requirement for structural precision in music is what sets the Classical era apart.Joseph Haydn, Ludwig van Beethoven, Franz Schubert, and Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart are just a few of the notable classical musicians.

Romantic (1820 – 1900)

The transition between the Classical and Romantic eras of music was made by Beethoven and Schubert. As implied by the term "romantic," this time period added obscene amounts of intensity and expression to classical music. As the era progressed, composers progressively shifted away from highly structured works and toward drama and emotion.

With orchestras becoming more numerous than ever, instrumentation became even more important. Composers explored new avenues of experimentation, utilizing unusual instrumentation combos and expanding the boundaries of harmony. The urban middle class society began to host public concerts and operas, bringing them out of the exclusive realm of monarchy and wealth.

20th Century and 21st Century (1900 – Current)

Even more discrete time eras can be created from the 20th and 21st centuries. Up until the 20th and 21st centuries, every age had a common set of rules and traits that the majority of composers adhered to. Composers have been moving away from guidelines and limitations over time, eventually arriving at a location where they are completely free to create. Even though it may not be as well-known today as it was in 1800, classical music is still a venue for the highest level of innovation.
